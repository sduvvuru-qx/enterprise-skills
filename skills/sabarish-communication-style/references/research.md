# Communication Research Synthesis

Use this reference when a message needs deeper editing, leadership sensitivity, or a durable style decision.

## Source-backed principles

### Plain language

From Digital.gov / PlainLanguage guidance:

- Write for the specific audience.
- Use active voice so responsibility is clear.
- Use present tense when possible.
- Prefer short sections and shorter words.
- Avoid hidden verbs such as `conduct an analysis` when `analyze` works.

### Technical writing clarity

From Google Technical Writing:

- Clarity is the top rule.
- Choose strong, specific verbs.
- Avoid vague starts like `There is` and `There are`.
- Each sentence should carry one idea.
- Convert long embedded lists into bullets only when that improves readability.
- Remove extraneous phrases: `at this point in time` -> `now`; `is able to` -> `can`.

### Scannability

From GOV.UK and Nielsen Norman Group:

- Busy readers skim.
- Put the most important information first.
- Use plain English even for specialist audiences.
- Keep writing brisk but human, serious but not pompous.
- Avoid long sentences, especially over 25 words.
- Use bullets for action lists, not for everything.

### Email etiquette

From Purdue OWL and workplace email norms:

- Use a meaningful subject line for new messages.
- In continuing threads, keep the reply in the original thread.
- Be direct and avoid wasting the recipient's time.
- Double-check To/Cc before replying, especially with reply all.
- Do not send sensitive passwords, credentials, or unnecessary confidential detail by email.
- Trim or avoid unnecessary old-message clutter when composing outside a normal thread.

## Astemo-specific rules

From local Astemo context:

- Use short engineering bullets.
- Lead with decision / ask / risk before background.
- Use tables only when tracking owners/actions.
- For Japanese counterpart asks, be precise and respectful; avoid idioms.
- For customer material, use supplier tone: factual, evidence-backed, no promises beyond known approvals.
- Draft only. Do not send unless the user explicitly approves, and in this workspace the user prefers to click Send manually.

## Recipient patterns

### Bryan Voris

- Department-level visibility and alignment.
- Lead with business/customer/gate/resource impact.
- Provide options or a specific support ask.
- Avoid unresolved technical noise.
- Avoid broad `what should we do?` questions.

### Kevin Butler

- Manager-level triage and execution.
- State bottom line, blocked items, owner, impact, and recommendation.
- Avoid vague pings such as `Let me know if you need updates`.

### JP counterparts

- Use `-san`.
- State purpose, intended audience/use, current status, suggested next step.
- Ask for routing confirmation before sensitive parallel follow-ups.

## Message quality test

A good Sabarish message passes all of these:

1. Can the recipient tell why they are receiving it in the first 2 lines?
2. Is the ask or next step explicit?
3. Is ownership clear where it matters?
4. Does it separate confirmed from pending?
5. Could it be forwarded without embarrassment?
6. Does it sound like Sabarish, not ChatGPT?
