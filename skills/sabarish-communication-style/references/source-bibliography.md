# Source Bibliography

Research sources used to build this skill. Use this when validating why a communication rule exists.

## Google Technical Writing

- Audience: https://developers.google.com/tech-writing/one/audience
  - Define audience by role and proximity to the topic.
  - Provide only the knowledge/skills the audience needs but lacks.
  - Avoid idioms and culturally specific references for international audiences.
- Active voice: https://developers.google.com/tech-writing/one/active-voice
  - Active voice clarifies actor, verb, and target.
  - Passive voice often hides responsibility.
- Clear sentences: https://developers.google.com/tech-writing/one/clear-sentences
  - Use strong verbs.
  - Reduce `there is` / `there are`.
  - Minimize vague adjectives/adverbs.
- Short sentences: https://developers.google.com/tech-writing/one/short-sentences
  - One sentence, one idea.
  - Convert long embedded lists to bullets when helpful.
  - Remove extraneous words.

## Plain language / government writing

- Digital.gov Plain Language: https://digital.gov/guides/plain-language/writing/
  - Use active voice.
  - Use present tense where possible.
  - Avoid hidden verbs.
- GOV.UK Writing for GOV.UK: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/content-design/writing-for-gov-uk
  - Meet user need.
  - Front-load important information.
  - Specialists still prefer plain English.
  - Keep writing brisk but human.
- GOV.UK Style Guide A to Z: https://www.gov.uk/guidance/style-guide/a-to-z
  - Use plain English.
  - Use bullets carefully.
  - Avoid unclear abbreviations and unnecessary jargon.

## Email etiquette and professional structure

- Purdue OWL Email Etiquette: https://owl.purdue.edu/owl/general_writing/academic_writing/email_etiquette.html
  - Use meaningful subject lines.
  - Be direct and concise.
  - Double-check recipients, especially reply-all contexts.
  - Avoid sensitive material that should not be forwarded.
- Grammarly Professional Email Guide: https://www.grammarly.com/blog/email-writing/how-to-write-a-professional-email/
  - Use task-focused structure.
  - Use clear subject, greeting, focused body, clear request, sign-off/signature.
  - Review recipients, attachments, tone, and errors before sending.

## Scannability / async work

- Nielsen Norman Group, succinct writing: https://www.nngroup.com/articles/be-succinct-writing-for-the-web/
  - People skim onscreen.
  - Write short, scannable, structured text.
- Atlassian Working Agreements: https://www.atlassian.com/team-playbook/plays/working-agreements
  - Set communication channel purpose.
  - Define escalation process.
  - Decide when async writing is better than synchronous meetings.
  - Clarify who creates, contributes, reviews, and by when.

## Japan / intercultural communication

- Japan-guide Names: https://www.japan-guide.com/e/e2271.html
  - Japanese name order is family name first.
  - `san` is neutral and usable in most situations.
  - Titles depend on social position and context.
- Japan-guide Business Cards: https://www.japan-guide.com/e/e2227.html
  - Business etiquette reflects respect and attention to order/presentation.
  - Cards may be arranged by seating order to remember names and positions.
- Cultural Atlas Japan Communication: https://culturalatlas.sbs.com.au/japanese-culture/japanese-culture-communication
  - Japanese communication can be indirect to preserve harmony and face.
  - Silence can signal respect or thought, not necessarily disagreement.
  - Respectful speech reflects hierarchy and formality.
